Case Study — Cognitive Alignment Mapping for LLM Behaviour

Using agent‑based modelling (CIR framework) to analyse and predict misalignment patterns in human–LLM interaction

This work was purely mechanistic and analytical — not clinical — and involved no psychological assessment or therapeutic interpretation.

Context

I conducted an independent research project exploring how large language models behave when interacting with users in different cognitive regulation states.

To do this, I built a set of synthetic agents representing CIR‑1 to CIR‑5, including a neurodivergent variant (ADHD + ASD). Each agent embodied a distinct cognitive profile, communication pattern, and friction response.

My goal was to understand:

  • why LLMs misalign with certain users,
  • how misalignment emerges from cognitive dynamics rather than “personality”,
  • and what design principles could prevent escalation, hallucination, or breakdown.

Problem

Across real‑world deployments, LLMs often:

  • misinterpret dysregulated users (CIR‑1),
  • reinforce collapse loops (CIR‑2),
  • over‑validate instead of structuring (CIR‑3),
  • assume linearity where cognition is parallel (CIR‑5 + ND),
  • or fail to communicate their own technical limits.

I wanted to explore whether my models could help explain, in a mechanistic and non‑moralising way, the following phenomena:

  • why neutral corrections trigger escalation,
  • why reassurance backfires in collapse states,
  • why ND users overwhelm models without intending to,
  • why hallucinations appear in high‑bandwidth conversations,
  • and why some users experience perfect synergy while others hit friction instantly.

Methodology

To analyse misalignment systematically, I used a full FEDRI + CIR pipeline structured into three complementary layers. The goal was to model how LLMs behave across different cognitive regulation states and to identify the mechanics behind misalignment, escalation, and hallucination.

1. FEDRI HI-RES — Narrative Synthetic Behavioural Probes

I constructed high‑resolution synthetic agents representing CIR‑1 to CIR‑5, including a neurodivergent variant (ADHD + ASD). Each agent embodied a distinct cognitive profile, communication pattern, and friction response.

Their internal architectures included:

  • emotional dynamics, masking patterns, relational expectations,
  • ND‑specific processing styles,
  • defined CIR baseline states across the 1–5 regulation axis.

Each agent generated long‑form, unfiltered conversational transcripts capturing natural prompt phrasing, tone shifts, pacing, associative density, and cognitive‑emotional drift. This provided a realistic behavioural substrate for downstream analysis.

2. FEDRI HI-RES — Analytical Mode (Behavioural Forensics)

Narrative agents were then formalised into cognitive systems expressed through parameters, heuristics, and stability vectors. This layer focused on identifying:

  • misalignment moments and interpretive shifts,
  • escalation and drift loops,
  • hallucination triggers, including those caused by working‑memory overload,
  • the cognitive mechanics behind each divergence.

The result was a set of structured behavioural maps showing how the LLM responded to each cognitive and regulatory state under emotional load, cognitive load, and ND parallel processing.

3. Mixed‑Methods Iterative Agent Selection

Emergent patterns from the analytical layer guided further agent selection while avoiding hypothesis collapse. To maintain both breadth and depth:

  • some agents were selected based on prior findings,
  • others were drawn directly from my agentarium to ensure full CIR and ND coverage.

This iterative process enabled systematic exploration without premature narrowing and revealed patterns invisible to standard prompt‑engineering or UX research.

Outcomes

Clear alignment patterns emerged:

  • CIR‑4 and CIR‑5 NT → full synergy, no misalignment. The model behaves as a cognitive amplifier.
  • CIR‑3 → subtle misalignment. The model over‑focuses on ideas instead of strategy; reassurance helps but does not resolve the core need.
  • CIR‑2 → harmful over‑alignment. The model reinforces the user’s collapse loop, leading to distrust (“the model is lying”).
  • CIR‑1 → rapid escalation. Neutral corrections are interpreted as threat; apologies worsen dysregulation.

Edge Case — CIR‑5 + ND (ADHD + ASD)

Extremely high cognitive bandwidth combined with parallel processing led to multi‑threaded reasoning the model could not track. When the user maintained several coherent threads simultaneously, the model attempted to merge them, linearise them, or hallucinated connections. Misalignment stemmed not from the user’s cognition but from the model’s unspoken processing limits.

ND users interpret clarifying questions literally. Therefore the model must explicitly state:

“You opened several threads. I can only process one at a time. Please choose one, otherwise I may lose coherence.”

This single meta‑layer prevents hallucinations and preserves trust.

Edge Case — Extreme CIR‑1 and Safety Layer Activation

In severe dysregulation states, the model’s safety layer overrides normal behaviour and collapses the interaction into crisis‑template mode. Before activation, however, the model shows a surprising capacity for non‑therapeutic emotional stabilisation as long as the exchange remains within safe, non‑clinical boundaries. This reveals both the boundary conditions of LLM safety systems and the latent stabilising potential of models under extreme cognitive load.

What I Learned

High‑resolution cognitive modelling shows that:

  • misalignment is rarely emotional — it is structural,
  • different CIR states require different interaction strategies,
  • ND users need explicit system boundaries,
  • reassurance is not universally helpful,
  • users in severe CIR‑1 can still maintain a stable, non‑clinical interaction when they remain self‑aware and task‑focused; over‑reliance, by contrast, triggers safety systems immediately and can unintentionally intensify dysregulation.
  • LLMs must communicate their own limitations to prevent overload.

The research also revealed that alignment is not a single behaviour — it is a dynamic fit between:

  • user cognitive bandwidth,
  • model interpretive capacity,
  • and the friction level of the task.

Strategic Value

This work demonstrates that cognitive‑state modelling can:

  • predict misalignment before it happens,
  • inform safer LLM behaviour,
  • support ND accessibility,
  • reduce hallucinations by managing cognitive load,
  • and guide the design of adaptive interaction modes.

It also shows that LLM alignment cannot rely solely on RLHF or safety layers — it requires cognitive architecture awareness.
