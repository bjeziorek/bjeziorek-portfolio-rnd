Full Case Study — Behaviour forensics

Applying FEDRI to Understand and De-escalate a Workplace Conflict Through Agent Based Reconstruction

This reconstruction was purely mechanistic and analytical — not clinical — and involved no psychological assessment or therapeutic interpretation.


Context

An enterprise was struggling with chronic stress, recurring interpersonal conflicts, and a lack of psychological safety. I was asked to help the team understand escalating tensions that existing organisational frameworks had not been able to address. The environment relied heavily on implicit norms and unmanaged emotional labour, which caused misunderstandings to escalate rapidly and left neurodivergent employees particularly vulnerable to misinterpretation. With no predefined methodology provided, I independently chose to analyse the situation through a neutral, mechanics‑based reconstruction of a conflict captured during a project meeting. This became the first real‑world application of FEDRI High‑Resolution Cognitive Simulation to human interactions.

Problem

A routine project meeting escalated into a heated argument between several team members. The conflict was recorded on Microsoft Teams. The escalation reflected deeper systemic issues:

  • misaligned communication styles,
  • chronic stress and emotional depletion,
  • masking behaviours,
  • unspoken needs and expectations,
  • and a lack of shared understanding of each other’s cognitive processes.
The team asked for a reconstruction that avoided blame, judgement, or HR framing. Because SPINE was not applied, the reconstruction focused on individual cognitive mechanics rather than the organisational pressures shaping them — a limitation that became clear only in retrospect.

Intervention Overview

I applied FEDRI as a cognitive and behavioural forensics tool to reconstruct the conflict minute by minute. The process included:

  • transcribing the meeting,
  • modelling each participant as an autonomous agent,
  • reconstructing internal states as computational representations, not psychological diagnoses using FEDRI’s fractal psyche model,
  • identifying emotional triggers and escalation loops,
  • mapping relational dynamics and cross agent influence,
  • and presenting a neutral, non moralising reconstruction.

Because this was the first application of FEDRI on real people, participants were not fully prepared for the depth of introspection the model would generate — a limitation that became clear in hindsight. The absence of SPINE’s preparatory and protective layers, especially System Grounding, contributed to this lack of readiness.

Methodology

1. Fractal Agent Reconstruction

Each participant was modelled with:

  • CIR baseline (emotional regulation level),
  • layered motivations and defence patterns,
  • cognitive load and energy levels,
  • relational expectations,
  • masking strategies,
  • and stress sensitive behavioural signatures.

One participant showed clear neurodivergent processing patterns (literal interpretation, delayed emotional signalling, atypical regulation). This was incorporated without naming or disclosing ND traits. These interpretations were computational and behavioural, not diagnostic. SPINE’s Neurocognition layer, which would normally guide pacing and framing for ND participants, was not yet part of the process.

2. High Resolution Scene Replay

The meeting was reconstructed as a sequence of micro events:

  • tone shifts,
  • interruptions,
  • pauses,
  • semantic misalignments,
  • micro expressions,
  • and escalation triggers.

For each moment, FEDRI generated:

  • internal emotional state as a computational reconstruction, not a psychological assessment,
  • cognitive interpretation,
  • impulse vs. regulation conflict,
  • predicted intention,
  • and cross agent influence.

3. Behavioural Signature Distillation

The reconstruction revealed:

  • escalation loops,
  • misinterpretation chains,
  • unmet needs (clarity, recognition, safety, autonomy),
  • masking failures,
  • and points where regulation broke down.

4. Neutral Presentation

The final output was a cold, analytical reconstruction focused on mechanics rather than judgement. This allowed participants to see themselves not as “wrong”, but as agents responding to pressure, misalignment, and unmet needs. However, without SPINE’s System Grounding, some participants interpreted the analysis as personal exposure rather than contextual explanation.

Reactions and Limitations

Because participants were not prepared for the depth of the analysis, reactions varied significantly:

  • Some initially felt exposed or irritated, but after reflection recognised the accuracy and neutrality of the reconstruction.
  • One neurodivergent participant needed several weeks to process the material but ultimately reported feeling understood without being singled out.
  • Two participants remained at CIR Level 1, responding with defensiveness and escalating aggression. For them, the analysis intensified the conflict rather than resolving it.
  • These divergent reactions highlighted an important limitation: high resolution cognitive forensics requires psychological readiness and informed consent. Without preparation, the intervention can be overwhelming. This readiness refers to cognitive and emotional capacity for introspective analysis, not clinical suitability.

The absence of SPINE — particularly System Grounding, Prediction, and Intervention Design — contributed to these outcomes and later informed ethical guidelines for applying FEDRI in organisational settings.

Outcome

Despite the challenges, the intervention produced several meaningful outcomes:

  • Participants who engaged with the reconstruction gained clarity about their own internal states and the intentions of others.
  • The team recognised how unintentional triggers were driving repeated conflicts.
  • The neurodivergent participant, initially considered for dismissal, was retained after demonstrating the ability to reach CIR Level 3 when given appropriate conditions.
  • The two employees who consistently escalated conflict remained at CIR Level 1 despite multiple follow up interventions. These were organisational and behavioural interventions, not clinical ones. Their persistent aggressive patterns informed later managerial decisions.
  • Leadership reported that the analysis provided a structured, evidence based understanding of dynamics they had previously sensed but could not articulate.

Why the Approach Was Effective

FEDRI offered:

  • psychologically realistic agent modelling,
  • emergent relational dynamics,
  • high resolution emotional tracking,
  • neutrality and non moralising framing,
  • clarity through mechanical explanation rather than opinion,
  • and a structured way to differentiate between stress driven behaviour and persistent aggression.

When people understand the mechanics of behaviour, they often self correct — provided they can access higher CIR levels. CIR levels describe regulatory load, not mental health status. The later application of SPINE clarified why some participants could not.

Competencies Demonstrated

This case demonstrates:

  • cognitive reverse engineering,
  • agent based modelling of real human behaviour,
  • high resolution emotional reconstruction,
  • conflict forensics,
  • high load state informed analysis,
  • neurodivergent aware modelling,
  • emergent behaviour prediction,
  • and system level behavioural adjustment.

It also demonstrates the importance of ethical considerations, participant preparation, and awareness of psychological readiness — principles later formalised through full SPINE integration.

Strategic Value

This organisational and behavioural intervention, not clinical ones, showed that FEDRI is not only a theoretical cognitive architecture but an operational tool capable of:

  • mapping systemic dysfunction,
  • supporting neurodivergent employees,
  • improving team dynamics,
  • informing managerial decisions,
  • and transforming organisational behaviour through clarity rather than judgement.

It also highlighted the need for careful framing, preparation, and ethical safeguards when applying high resolution cognitive forensics in real world environments. The later adoption of the full SPINE framework addressed these gaps and now forms the foundation for all subsequent applications.

This was an analytical reconstruction, not a clinical or therapeutic intervention.

Where SPINE Was Not Fully Applied — and What It Revealed

The intervention produced meaningful outcomes and resolved the recurring conflict, but it was also the first real world application of FEDRI. In practice, only two components of the broader SPINE framework were applied: Cognitive Modelling and Behavioural Friction Mapping. These layers were strong enough to clarify team dynamics and eliminate repeated tensions, yet the absence of the remaining SPINE components—especially System Grounding—shaped the experience in ways that became clear only afterward.

Impact of Missing System Grounding

System Grounding is the first stage of SPINE and provides the contextual foundation for all cognitive and behavioural analysis. It maps structural pressures, cultural norms, workflow constraints, and environmental stressors that shape behaviour long before individual psychology becomes visible.

Because the reconstruction was not explicitly grounded in the organisation’s high stress culture, legacy tooling, unclear norms, and chronic overload, the analysis focused primarily on individual cognitive states rather than the systemic forces driving them. This led to several outcomes:

  • Participants at low regulation (CIR 1) interpreted the analysis as personal exposure rather than contextual explanation. Without a system level frame, the reconstruction felt like a spotlight on their behaviour instead of a map of the pressures acting on them.
  • Two participants remained in defensive or aggressive patterns and escalated rather than de‑escalated. Their reactions aligned with individuals experiencing high system pressure that is not acknowledged.
  • The neurodivergent participant required significantly more time to process the analysis because the missing system context made the experience feel more exposing than supportive.
  • The organisational culture—the true root cause—remained unaddressed, limiting the long term potential of the intervention.

The analysis itself was accurate, but without System Grounding it lacked the protective framing that helps people understand why they behaved as they did, rather than feeling that they were the problem.

What Full SPINE Application Might Have Changed

Had the full SPINE framework been applied, several outcomes might have differed:

  • System Grounding would have clarified that many behaviours were predictable responses to structural stress rather than personal flaws, reducing defensiveness and helping more participants reach CIR 3.
  • Prediction would have made it possible to anticipate who was likely to escalate and to design a gentler or staged intervention for them.
  • Intervention Design would have introduced safety mechanisms, pacing, and preparatory framing to prevent overwhelm.
  • Neurocognition would have highlighted the need for different processing timelines and emotional buffers for neurodivergent participants.
  • Environment Validation would have revealed that organisational culture was a major driver of conflict, enabling a broader systemic intervention rather than focusing solely on individuals.

With full SPINE, the two employees who remained at CIR 1 might have been supported more effectively, and the organisation might have gained earlier insight into the cultural patterns generating conflict.

What I Learned

This experience reshaped how I apply cognitive and behavioural forensics in real world environments:

  • High resolution analysis is powerful, but without system context it can feel personal and overwhelming.
  • People need to understand the pressures acting on them before they can reflect on their own behaviour.
  • Cognitive Modelling without System Grounding can unintentionally reinforce a sense of blame.
  • Neurodivergent participants require explicit framing and pacing to ensure emotional safety.
  • SPINE is not optional—it is the ethical backbone that ensures cognitive forensics supports people rather than destabilising them.

The intervention succeeded in resolving the immediate conflict, but it also demonstrated that no cognitive model is complete without the system that shapes it, and no behavioural analysis is safe without the environment that contextualises it.

Ethical Reflections — Responsibility, Impact, and the Limits of Partial SPINE Application

This was the first real world use of FEDRI on human interactions, and it revealed an important ethical dimension: applying only Cognitive Modelling and Behavioural Friction Mapping, without the stabilising layers of SPINE, amplified the impact of high resolution analysis in ways that were not fully predictable.

When High Resolution Analysis Meets Human Vulnerability

FEDRI’s reconstruction exposed internal cognitive and emotional mechanisms with a level of precision participants were not prepared for. For individuals at low emotional regulation (CIR 1), the absence of System Grounding made the analysis feel personal, exposing, or overwhelming. Without a clear framing of organisational pressures, some interpreted the reconstruction as commentary on who they were rather than on the system they were reacting to.

Two participants remained in defensive or aggressive patterns, and the analysis unintentionally intensified their sense of threat. Their reactions were predictable outcomes of high system pressure combined with low regulation and insufficient contextual framing.

What I Learned About Responsibility in Cognitive Forensics

This experience showed that high resolution cognitive analysis is not neutral by default—it is powerful, and power requires responsibility. Key lessons included:

  • System context is essential; without it, individuals may feel blamed for behaviours shaped by structural pressures.
  • Emotional safety must be engineered through preparation, framing, and pacing.
  • Not everyone can access reflection at the same time; people at CIR 1 require stabilisation before insight.
  • Neurodivergent processing timelines must be respected, with more time and buffering for integration.
  • Interventions can be effective and still cause unintended harm; organisational success does not erase individual emotional impact.

Why This Experience Strengthened My Commitment to SPINE

SPINE exists to prevent exactly these unintended consequences. It ensures that:

  • behaviour is interpreted within systemic and environmental context,
  • predictions account for emotional readiness,
  • interventions include safety mechanisms,
  • neurocognitive differences are integrated into design,
  • and real world conditions are validated before applying high resolution tools.

Had SPINE been applied in full, the two participants who remained at CIR 1 might have experienced reduced defensiveness and greater capacity for reflection. It might also have surfaced cultural drivers of conflict long before individual behaviour became the focus.

How This Experience Shapes My Work Going Forward

The intervention resolved the conflict and clarified team dynamics, but it also highlighted the ethical responsibility inherent in cognitive forensics. It reinforced that:

  • Insight without grounding can destabilise.
  • Precision without context can feel like exposure.
  • Analysis without preparation can overwhelm.
  • Powerful tools require full frameworks, not partial application.

SPINE is not just a methodology—it is a safeguard. It ensures that cognitive modelling supports people rather than destabilising them, and that behavioural forensics is applied with care, context, and respect for human vulnerability.

This experience strengthened my commitment to ethical cognitive engineering and shaped the safeguards I now use to ensure psychological safety in all future interventions.
