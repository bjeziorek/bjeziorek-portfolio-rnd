Case Study — MAKO / Maths in New Clothes

Behavioural learning system for schools: increasing engagement and reducing emotional load through cognitive modelling and behavioural interventions.

Context

Behavioural Learning System in a Real School Environment

MAKO was a two‑year behavioural research initiative that evolved into the grant‑winning product “Maths in New Clothes”, implemented in a real school environment with over 120 students. The goal was to increase engagement, reduce stress, and improve learning outcomes without changing the curriculum or extending lesson time.

Problem

Inconsistent Engagement and Emotional Overload

Students showed inconsistent engagement, high avoidance, and emotional overload. Teachers reported that motivation fluctuated unpredictably and that traditional engagement strategies were ineffective.

How I Applied SPINE

SPINE in MAKO / Maths in New Clothes

Systems - Structural Constraints Before Motivation

I began by mapping the school environment: fixed lesson times, curriculum constraints, teacher workload, and the emotional climate of the classroom. This revealed that many behavioural issues were structural rather than motivational.


Prediction - Cognitive Models of Student Profiles

I built cognitive models for different student profiles (high anxiety, ND, low energy, trauma affected, high achieving but stressed). These models predicted that stress, not lack of ability, was the main barrier to engagement.

During the modelling phase, I used CIR and FEDRI to map regulation load, avoidance loops, overwhelm thresholds, and the internal state transitions that drive engagement or withdrawal. These models helped predict when students would disengage not because of ability, but because their cognitive system entered a protective state.


Intervention - Reframing Maths Through a Meta Layer

Instead of changing the maths tasks themselves, I built a meta layer that changed how students approached the tasks. The system reframed maths from a punishment‑driven activity into a strategy choice: students could follow the traditional grading path (fear‑based) or the MAKO path (progress‑based). Both paths required doing the same exercises, but the meaning wrapped around them was radically different.

I neutralised the emotional penalty of grades by replacing immediate grading with a points‑based currency that converted into a grade only at the end of an event. This removed anxiety and restored a sense of control. I also redesigned the cost of mistakes: wrong answers were rewarded with points, turning “being wrong” from a social risk into a safe, rewarded attempt. This single shift unlocked engagement from students who had previously stayed silent out of fear.

The intervention didn’t motivate through rewards alone — it reframed the entire experience. By wrapping boring tasks in structure, progression, and meaning, the system made students willingly engage with work they previously avoided. The result was a behavioural transformation driven not by coercion, but by a redesigned cognitive and emotional environment.

The intervention design was grounded in the mechanisms identified through CIR and FEDRI: reducing the cognitive cost of mistakes, lowering regulation load, and interrupting avoidance loops. By altering the emotional meaning of tasks rather than the tasks themselves, the system stabilised engagement even for students with high sensitivity to error or performance pressure.


Neurocognition - Designing for Emotional Load & ND Profiles

I analysed how emotional load, avoidance, and working memory limitations affected learning. This helped me design interventions that were safe and effective for both neurotypical and neurodivergent students.


Real‑World Validation

All interventions were tested in real classrooms under real constraints. I iterated based on behavioural data, teacher feedback, and student responses.

The system was stress‑tested under real‑world constraints: fluctuating emotional climates, inconsistent attention, high‑pressure lesson pacing, and unpredictable shifts in student regulation. This ensured that MAKO remained stable even when the environment was noisy, chaotic, or emotionally charged.

Impact

Behavioural and Learning Outcomes

  • Significant increase in engagement and attendance.
  • Noticeable improvement in learning outcomes.
  • Reduced stress and avoidance behaviours.
  • Teachers reported more stable classroom dynamics.
  • The system became a reference point for other classes in the school.

MAKO demonstrated that cognitive modelling and behavioural interventions can meaningfully improve learning even in highly constrained environments. It showed that reframing and cognitive modelling can outperform traditional motivational strategies even in rigid, constraint‑heavy environments.
