Case Study — Hi-Res Cognitive Forensics in an Enterprise Environment

Using FEDRI to reconstruct and de‑escalate a workplace conflict through agent‑based modelling

This reconstruction was purely mechanistic and analytical — not clinical — and involved no psychological assessment or therapeutic interpretation.

Context

An enterprise was experiencing chronic stress, recurring interpersonal conflicts, and low psychological safety. I was brought in without a predefined methodology or expected approach. Based on the available material, I independently chose to conduct a neutral, mechanics‑based reconstruction of a conflict captured during a project meeting

Problem

A routine meeting escalated into a heated argument. Traditional HR tools failed to explain why:

  • communication collapsed within seconds,
  • participants reacted defensively to neutral cues,
  • neurodivergent employees were misinterpreted,
  • and the same conflict pattern kept repeating.

The team needed a non‑moralising explanation of what actually happened.

How I Applied FEDRI

I used FEDRI as a cognitive forensics tool:

  • transcribed the meeting and reconstructed it minute‑by‑minute,
  • modelled each participant as an autonomous agent with CIR levels, cognitive load, masking, and relational expectations,
  • analysed micro‑events (tone shifts, pauses, interruptions, semantic misalignment),
  • generated computational reconstructions of internal states (not diagnoses),
  • mapped escalation loops, unmet needs, and cross‑agent influence.

This was the first real‑world use of FEDRI on human interactions — applied without the full SPINE framework, which later proved to be a critical limitation.

Outcomes

Despite missing SPINE:

  • participants with higher regulation (CIR 3) recognised their own patterns and self‑corrected,
  • the neurodivergent employee — previously considered for dismissal — was retained after the model revealed stable regulation under the right conditions,
  • leadership gained a clear, evidence‑based map of team dynamics,
  • recurring conflicts stopped.

However:

  • two participants at CIR 1 reacted defensively; without System Grounding, the analysis felt exposing rather than contextual,
  • ND participants needed more time and framing to process the depth of the reconstruction,
  • organisational culture — the true root cause — remained initially invisible.

What I Learned

This experience revealed that high‑resolution cognitive forensics is powerful but not neutral:

  • without system context, precision can feel like blame,
  • people must understand structural pressures before reflecting on their own behaviour,
  • CIR 1 requires stabilisation before insight is possible,
  • ND processing timelines require explicit pacing and emotional buffers,
  • partial application of FEDRI can unintentionally overwhelm.

This led to the formalisation of SPINE as the ethical backbone for all future interventions.

Strategic Value

The intervention demonstrated that FEDRI can:

  • map systemic dysfunction,
  • support neurodivergent employees,
  • clarify relational dynamics,
  • inform managerial decisions,
  • and transform behaviour through mechanical clarity rather than judgement.

It also established the need for full SPINE integration — ensuring grounding, prediction, safety mechanisms, neurocognitive considerations, and environmental validation before applying high‑resolution analysis.

Competencies Demonstrated

  • agent‑based modelling of real human behaviour
  • cognitive reverse engineering
  • high‑resolution emotional reconstruction
  • ND‑aware behavioural analysis
  • escalation loop mapping
  • conflict forensics
  • ethical cognitive engineering
  • system‑level behavioural insight
