Dishwashing System Reconstruction

Case study - Kitchen Emergent Life

The article begins with a familiar scenario: entering a deep flow state for hours, ignoring basic chores, and eventually discovering a thriving “emergent life form colony” in the kitchen sink. Instead of treating this as a moral failure or a personality flaw, the author applies her cognitive R&D frameworks — SPINE, FEDRI, and CIR — to analyse the situation as if it were a complex behavioural system.

1. System Grounding (SPINE Step 1)

The author maps the system:

  • User: exhausted, low motivation, strong aversion to boring tasks.
  • Environment: tiny, ergonomically hostile kitchen.
  • System: no protocols, high entropy, and a sink that triggers avoidance.

2. Cognitive Modelling (SPINE Step 2 + CIR + FEDRI)

Using CIR, she identifies that she operates at CIR 3 after long flow sessions — functional but depleted. FEDRI simulations reveal that dishwashing triggers forced state dysregulation:

  • forced → avoidance → overwhelm → collapse → emergent life.

3. Behavioural Friction Mapping (SPINE Step 3)

She identifies friction points:

  • the material of the kitchen sink (textured metal)
  • tasks longer than a few minutes
  • low CIR
  • water being wet
  • lack of a protocol
  • sensory mismatch between objects and tasks

Surprisingly, the bathroom sink — smooth white porcelain — creates far less friction, explaining why she can wash dishes there but not in the kitchen.

4. Hypothesis Architecture (SPINE Step 4)

Initial hypothesis: wash dishes in the bathroom to eliminate the colony. But deeper analysis reveals the real issue: object identity and sensory assignments. Certain cups have dedicated roles (juice cup, coffee cup, etc.), and violating these roles creates cognitive friction.

5. High Resolution FEDRI Simulations

She tests what would happen if she used any cup for any drink. Result:

  • avoidance of dishwashing and avoidance of drinking
  • collapse of the “organism wants juice → organism gets juice” behavioural loop

Conclusion: Reducing the number of cups makes the system worse. Increasing the number of dedicated cups stabilises it.

6. Systemic Implementation (SPINE Step 6)

She designs a full environmental reset:

  • empty the cupboard
  • identify natural behavioural assignments
  • assign one vessel per drink/food type
  • reorganise the cupboard to reduce friction
  • remove entropy generating elements (like the giant plastic drying rack)
  • optionally keep dedicated vessels permanently on the drainer

7. Systemic Rationale

What looked like chaos was actually a hidden behavioural architecture:

  • the system had functional mappings all along
  • the user kept breaking them with “logical” but incompatible rules
  • the environment was an unindexed database, not a mess
  • the system had been self correcting (via “weird cup purchases”) for years

Once the environment was reset, the architecture became visible — and suddenly the author felt motivated to maintain the system.

8. Field Update

After implementation:

  • emergent life annihilated
  • kitchen stabilised
  • discovered she owns 25 cups, not 13
  • realised she finally believes a clean kitchen is possible
  • the system now works with her physiology instead of against it

9. Final Insight

The solution wasn’t discipline, motivation, or minimalism. It was aligning the environment with the cognitive system. Once the architecture was revealed, everything made sense — and the system became self maintaining.
