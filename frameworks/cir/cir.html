CIR — Regulation & Stability Model

CIR FRAMEWORK

CIR is a five‑level model of cognitive stability that describes how emotional bandwidth, executive function, and behavioural control fluctuate under stress, overload, or dysregulation. It was originally developed as an introspective framework and later became the stability axis within my broader cognitive‑systems architecture.

The model incorporates neuromodulatory dynamics (e.g., cortisol, dopamine, oxytocin, prolactin) and gating mechanisms that shape how cognitive resources are allocated under load.

CIR is used for modelling cognitive failure modes, emotional‑load dynamics, behavioural prediction, and stability‑aware system design.

CIR is not a diagnostic or therapeutic tool; it is a computational and mechanistic model of regulation that describes system dynamics rather than clinical conditions. The model has theoretical relevance for understanding patterns that appear in clinical contexts, but it is not a clinical tool and does not replace diagnosis or treatment.

Origin

CIR originated during my early training in emergency medicine, where I studied — among other disciplines — psychology, psychiatry in the context of high‑stress environments. During clinical placements I observed a recurring misalignment between how healthcare systems operate and how people function when they are under acute stress. Individuals in crisis do not simply “function worse” — they function differently, with distinct patterns of gating, resource allocation, and information processing. In an environment where unpredictability is the norm, I wanted at least one predictable element: a mechanistic way to understand how cognitive stability degrades under load.

When I later moved into the game and med‑tech space, these early insights became the foundation for the first version of CIR. In 2012, CIR took the form of a lightweight four‑level cognitive‑regulation model developed for a psychoeducational RPG‑style application. The system used narrative progression and in‑game feedback to help users map their own psychological state fluctuations and learn to recognise patterns in their regulation. The project was evaluated by Krakow Technology Park at 800,000 PLN (~$200,000) investment readiness, with a projected 6M PLN (~$1.5M) development budget.

The early version of CIR focused on modelling dysregulation states and did not yet include neuromodulatory or biochemical mechanisms. CIR‑5 (the enhanced‑stability state) was added shortly after the initial release, expanding the model beyond dysregulation mapping and establishing the full five‑level stability continuum. Over the following years, the framework evolved organically as new layers were added, including neuromodulation, gating mechanisms, and system‑level resource‑allocation dynamics.

Today’s CIR represents a significantly expanded and more computationally precise architecture than its original 2012 form, integrating cognitive dynamics, neuromodulation, and multi‑layered regulation processes into a unified stability model.

Structure of the Model

CIR (Continuum · Introspection · Regulation) CIR defines five regulation states ranging from severe dysregulation to enhanced stability. The model is not clinical; it is a mechanistic map of how cognitive states shift and how those shifts influence perception and decision‑making.

CIR-1 Breakdown State (Severe Dysregulation)

A state of extreme destabilisation in which emotional regulation and behavioural control are essentially unavailable.

CIR‑1 is a breakdown state marked by complete loss of executive control and threat‑driven, fragmented cognition, where behaviour becomes purely reactive and functional decision‑making is impossible. The linked section outlines how this extreme dysregulation disrupts perception, information processing, and behavioural stability.

CIR-2 — Collapse State (High Dysregulation)

A state of significant instability in which control is fragile and easily collapses.

CIR‑2 is a high‑dysregulation collapse state in which executive control is unstable, information processing becomes emotion‑driven, and even mild stress can trigger withdrawal or shutdown. This mode of functioning is characterised by limited access to regulation, fear‑biased interpretations, and intermittent but unreliable cognitive control.

CIR-3 — Fragile Stability State (Compensated Regulation)

A state of forced stabilisation in which functioning is possible but requires substantial effort.

CIR‑3 is a fragile‑stability state in which a person can function, but only through sustained cognitive effort, with limited flexibility and rapid fatigue. This mode of functioning is characterised by regulation that is technically intact yet energetically costly, leading to slowed processing, rigid thinking, and reduced cognitive reserve.

CIR-4 — Stable Regulation State (Baseline Stability)

A state of healthy regulation in which functioning is smooth and balanced.

CIR‑4 is a baseline stability state in which regulation is smooth, executive functions are fully available, and cognition integrates emotion and logic without strain. This mode of functioning is characterised by balanced processing, reliable behavioural control, adaptive stress regulation, and consistent goal‑directed motivation.

CIR‑5 — Enhanced Stability State (Optimised Regulation)

A state of elevated stability in which the individual has access to full cognitive and emotional reserve.

CIR‑5 is an enhanced‑stability, peak‑performance state in which cognitive and emotional resources are fully available, enabling creative reasoning, strategic thinking, and highly adaptive stress regulation. This mode of functioning is marked by resilience, flexible information processing, intrinsic motivation, and expanded metacognition.

Why CIR Matters

CIR makes it possible to design systems that don’t assume an ideal, always‑stable user, but instead account for real drops in cognitive stability and the resulting distortions in reasoning.

Most systems implicitly assume that users operate in stable states (Levels 3–4). In reality, people frequently drop to Levels 1–2 for short periods due to stress, frustration, fear, or cognitive overload. CIR provides a structured way to understand:

  • why misinterpretations occur under pressure
  • how information processing changes across stability levels
  • when reasoning becomes constrained or distorted
  • how behaviour shifts during dysregulation

This makes CIR a useful conceptual tool for analysing breakdowns and misunderstandings in human–system interactions.

Applications in My Work

CIR functions as a stability layer across my frameworks:

  • In FEDRI, it defines the depth of simulation and governs transitions between cognitive states.
  • In SPINE, it helps map cognitive load and stability in real‑world environments.
  • In product and UX contexts, it is used selectively when modelling users who experience emotional volatility, stress, or overload.

Potential Applications of CIR

  • Human–system interaction — analysing why users misinterpret information or lose reasoning capacity under stress, overload, or emotional volatility.
  • Cognitive‑inspired modelling — providing a stability axis for simulations, agent behaviour, and systems that need to reason about fluctuating cognitive resources.
  • Mental‑health‑relevant dynamics — mapping overload, shutdown, grief, burnout, and neurodivergent stability patterns in a non‑clinical, mechanistic way.
  • Alignment and misalignment analysis — explaining how drops in cognitive stability distort interpretation and why misunderstandings emerge in high‑load contexts.
  • Behavioural prediction under pressure — modelling how decision‑making shifts when executive function becomes constrained or reactive.
  • High‑load environments — supporting reasoning about behaviour in learning contexts, high‑stakes decision scenarios, or emotionally demanding situations.

Relationship to the Broader Architecture

CIR provides the vertical axis of stability, FEDRI provides the horizontal axis of cognitive and emotional dynamics, and SPINE provides the environmental grounding. Together, they form a coherent stack for modelling behaviour in complex, high‑load, or unstable conditions.
