CIR — Regulation & Stability Model - Levels

CIR LEVELS

CIR Levels define five functional states of a cognitive system under varying regulatory load. Each level reflects a distinct configuration of available cognitive resources, stability mechanisms, and information‑processing capacity. The model provides a structured way to analyse how systems behave when regulation weakens or strengthens — from breakdown states to optimised stability. Rather than describing traits, the levels map dynamic operating modes that inform system design, resilience engineering, and intervention strategies aimed at maintaining or restoring functional stability.

CIR Levels

CIR-1 Breakdown State (Severe Dysregulation)

  • Cognitive resources — minimal; primitive reactions dominate, no access to executive functions.
  • Behavioural control — lost; impulsivity, abrupt reactions, inability to inhibit responses.
  • Information processing — fragmented and distorted; stimuli interpreted as threats.
  • Susceptibility to overload — extreme; any additional stimulus worsens the state.
  • Interpretation patterns — catastrophising, hostile attributions, threat perception even in neutral signals.
  • Typical behaviours — aggression, self‑aggression, panic, sensory overreactivity; aggression and crying can co‑occur because both are downstream effects of extreme sympathetic activation combined with the onset of prolactin‑mediated reset.
  • Metacognition — absent; no capacity to observe or modulate internal states.
  • Motivation / drive — reactive impulses; no goal‑directed behaviour.
  • Characteristic cognitive biases — hostile attribution bias, emotional reasoning, black‑and‑white threat perception.
  • Gating profile — total gating collapse; inhibitory control fails, all inputs are threat‑tagged, and the system cannot filter or prioritise signals.
  • Neuromodulatory signature — extremely high cortisol and noradrenaline with near‑zero serotonin; oxytocin is absent; when overload becomes unsustainable, the system triggers a prolactin‑driven distress reset after intense crying, enabling minimal recovery toward CIR‑2.
  • Memory bandwidth — none; available memory is consumed by subconscious threat‑evaluation processes, with resources severely limited by HPA‑axis overload.
  • Functionality in the real world — nearly impossible; the individual cannot perform tasks or make decisions.

CIR-2 — Collapse State (High Dysregulation)

A state of significant instability in which control is fragile and easily collapses.

  • Cognitive resources — limited; access to executive functions is intermittent.
  • Behavioural control — fragile; short periods of control are possible but easily disrupted.
  • Information processing — selective and emotion‑dominated; difficulty assessing situations.
  • Susceptibility to overload — very high; overload leads to withdrawal or collapse.
  • Interpretation patterns — fear‑based, pessimistic, threat‑oriented interpretations.
  • Typical behaviours — crying, avoidance, freezing, apathy, low‑energy withdrawal patterns, hypersensitivity.
  • Metacognition — fragmented; occasional insight but no ability to act on it.
  • Motivation / drive — avoidance‑based; withdrawal, shutdown, or passive coping.
  • Characteristic cognitive biases — catastrophising, overgeneralisation, negative filtering.
  • Gating profile — unstable, oscillatory gating; the system alternates between hyper‑permeability (flooding) and shutdown (blocking).
  • Neuromodulatory signature — prolactin remains elevated, supporting the “grief‑reset” state; cortisol is still high but begins to plateau; serotonin remains low; oxytocin stays minimal unless external relational cues appear.
  • Memory bandwidth — minimal; most capacity is consumed by subconscious threat‑evaluation processes, with resources constrained by HPA‑axis overload.
  • Functionality in the real world — significantly limited; the individual can perform simple tasks but cannot maintain stability.

CIR-3 — Fragile Stability State (Compensated Regulation)

A state of forced stabilisation in which functioning is possible but requires substantial effort.

  • Cognitive resources — moderate; executive functions operate but are energetically costly.
  • Behavioural control — maintained through effort; regulatory fatigue occurs easily.
  • Information processing — accurate but slowed; difficulty handling complex stimuli.
  • Susceptibility to overload — moderate; overload triggers a drop to Level 2.
  • Interpretation patterns — neutral or slightly negative; limited ability for positive reinterpretation.
  • Typical behaviours — functioning “on force”, reduced joy, sense of meaninglessness, survival mode.
  • Metacognition — present but effortful; the individual can self‑monitor but cannot easily adjust.
  • Motivation / drive — task‑oriented but low‑energy; focus on obligations rather than goals.
  • Characteristic cognitive biases — negative bias, reduced cognitive flexibility, rigid thinking.
  • Gating profile — functional but high‑cost gating; the system filters inputs through forced inhibitory control, resulting in slow, effortful regulation.
  • Neuromodulatory signature — moderate cortisol with unstable dopamine; oxytocin begins to rise only in safe relational contexts; serotonin partially recovers but remains fragile.
  • Memory bandwidth — limited; cognitive resources remain constrained due to residual HPA‑axis load.
  • Functionality in the real world — sufficient; the individual operates but without cognitive reserve.

CIR-4 — Stable Regulation State (Baseline Stability)

A state of healthy regulation in which functioning is smooth and balanced.

  • Cognitive resources — full; access to planning, reflection, and cognitive flexibility.
  • Behavioural control — stable; reactions are appropriate and proportional.
  • Information processing — balanced; emotions and logic can be integrated.
  • Susceptibility to overload — low; stress is regulated adaptively.
  • Interpretation patterns — realistic and balanced; capacity for positive and neutral interpretation.
  • Typical behaviours — healthy sensitivity, appropriate boundaries, relational capacity, experience of joy.
  • Metacognition — stable; the individual can observe, evaluate, and adjust internal states.
  • Motivation / drive — consistent; ability to pursue goals and maintain routines.
  • Characteristic cognitive biases — balanced appraisal, contextual reasoning, flexible thinking.
  • Gating profile — efficient, selective gating; inhibitory and excitatory pathways are balanced, enabling smooth filtering and flexible attention.
  • Neuromodulatory signature — balanced dopamine, serotonin, and stable oxytocin support adaptive regulation, social engagement, and cognitive flexibility.
  • Memory bandwidth — full; cognitive resources are fully available.
  • Functionality in the real world — full; the individual functions effectively and stably.

CIR‑5 — Enhanced Stability State (Optimised Regulation)

A state of elevated stability in which the individual has access to full cognitive and emotional reserve.

  • Cognitive resources — very high; creativity, strategic thinking, broad perspective.
  • Behavioural control — strengthened; high resistance to stress and pressure.
  • Information processing — fast, flexible, multi‑layered; ability to integrate complex data.
  • Susceptibility to overload — very low; stress is processed adaptively without loss of function.
  • Interpretation patterns — constructive, grounded in agency and meaning.
  • Typical behaviours — courage in pursuing goals, humour, self‑irony, high psychological resilience.
  • Metacognition — expanded; strong self‑awareness, strategic self‑regulation, long‑term perspective.
  • Motivation / drive — proactive; high initiative, intrinsic motivation, long‑range goal pursuit.
  • Characteristic cognitive biases — constructive reframing, optimism grounded in realism, high cognitive flexibility.
  • Gating profile — optimally tuned gating; high‑bandwidth selective updating, rapid integration, and minimal energetic cost.
  • Neuromodulatory signature — endorphins differentiate CIR‑5 from CIR‑4 by adding analgesia, elevated mood, and increased resilience; dopamine and oxytocin are high, cortisol is low, and serotonin is stable.
  • Memory bandwidth - extended; enhanced by endorphin‑ and dopamine‑supported cognitive efficiency.
  • Functionality in the real world —very high; the individual functions not only stably but also creatively and proactively.
