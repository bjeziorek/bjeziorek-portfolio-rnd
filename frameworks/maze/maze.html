MAZE — Directions mapping system

MAZE Framework

MAZE is a multinarrative, fractal mapping system that represents the directions a person can take in life — from immediate micro‑actions to long‑range identity‑level trajectories. It provides a structural map of goals, obligations, desires, dreams, and social expectations, all expressed as branching pathways that can expand or collapse depending on context, emotional state, and cognitive resources.

Where CIR models stability, and FEDRI simulates cognition and behaviour, MAZE provides the narrative‑directional landscape on which identity and behaviour unfold. It is the map that IMI (the identity and emotional‑memory engine) will navigate, and the structural layer that allows FEDRI agents to reason about long‑term direction, not just moment‑to‑moment behaviour.

MAZE does not prescribe what a person should do — it reveals the structure of what is possible, desired, feared, or expected, and how these directions interact across time.

Origin

MAZE began as a set of adaptive heuristics developed for SelfScape, a neurodivergent‑friendly planning system designed for users who struggle with linear calendars, fluctuating energy, and emotional overload. Early prototypes were validated in real ND daily conditions and proved that a fractal, narrative‑based task structure could reduce avoidance and support users through irregular routines, shutdowns, and hyperfocus cycles. A later project in cognitive‑load management for language learning showed that MAZE generalises beyond ND contexts, revealing its broader potential as a universal life‑direction mapping system. This led to its formalisation as a standalone framework and its integration into the larger cognitive architecture, where MAZE now serves as the directional layer for FEDRI and the structural foundation for IMI.

Structure of the Model

MAZE (Multinarrative • Adaptive • Zoomable • Emotion‑Driven) A dynamic life‑direction mapping system that models how goals, obligations, desires, and identity trajectories branch, evolve, and interact across time.

What MAZE Represents

What MAZE Represents MAZE begins with a starting point — a moment in time, a psychological state, or a narrative snapshot — and generates a branching structure of life directions:

  • I should
  • I must
  • I want
  • I wish
  • I dream
  • I fear
  • I owe
  • I am expected to
Each direction becomes a branch, and each branch can recursively expand into finer layers of detail. This creates a fractal structure of life trajectories that can be zoomed in or out depending on the level of abstraction required.

Zoom Levels

MAZE supports multi‑scale navigation:

  • Zoom‑in (micro‑actions)
    • “Pass exam” → pick topic → pick book → open chapter → read paragraph
  • Zoom‑out (macro‑trajectories)
    • “Pass exam” → finish university → get stable job → buy house → build family
Every node in the structure is both:
  • a snapshot of intention,
  • and a gateway to deeper narrative layers.
This makes MAZE a temporal‑narrative map rather than a static to‑do list.

Temporal Dynamics

MAZE is not a static tree — it moves along a timeline. Each MAZE snapshot is timestamped, representing the state of a person’s life‑directions at a specific moment. Over time:

  • branches appear or disappear
  • priorities shift
  • emotional weight changes
  • obligations accumulate or resolve
  • dreams strengthen or fade
  • identity trajectories diverge or converge
MAZE becomes a living archive of directional evolution, allowing IMI and FEDRI to reason about how identity and behaviour change across months, years, or entire life arcs.

Coupling and Overlap

Life directions rarely exist in isolation. MAZE explicitly models coupling, where branches influence or constrain each other:

  • “Find love” ↔ “Buy house”
  • “Finish university” ↔ “Be socially accepted”
  • “Start a business” ↔ “Maintain financial stability”
  • “Care for family” ↔ “Pursue personal growth”
Coupling can be:
  • supportive (one direction enables another)
  • competitive (one direction blocks another)
  • neutral (they coexist without interaction)
This allows MAZE to represent the real complexity of human life, where goals interact rather than existing as isolated tasks.

Role in the Broader Architecture

MAZE provides the narrative‑directional layer of your cognitive‑systems stack:

  • CIR supplies the stability axis.
  • FEDRI simulates cognition, emotion, and behaviour.
  • MAZE provides the map of life directions and trajectories.
  • IMI (in development) will provide identity, memory, and continuity across time.
Together, they allow agents — fictional or real — to reason not only about what they feel or how they behave, but also where they are going and why.

Why MAZE Matters

MAZE enables modelling of:

  • long‑term identity trajectories
  • conflicts between goals, obligations, and desires
  • how emotional states reshape life directions
  • how stability (CIR) influences prioritisation
  • how agents in FEDRI choose between competing paths
  • how identity evolves across time
  • how dreams, fears, and expectations interact
It transforms directionality from a vague concept into a formal, navigable structure.
