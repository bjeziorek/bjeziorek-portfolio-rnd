MAZE Origin

MAZE began as a set of heuristics developed for SelfScape, a cognitive‑R&D project aimed at creating a planning system for neurodivergent users who struggle with linear calendars, time‑based pressure, and inconsistent energy levels. The early prototypes were not yet a formal architecture — they were adaptive rules designed to reduce avoidance, overwhelm, and shutdown in users whose cognitive and emotional states fluctuate throughout the day.

During the initial SPINE‑driven research phase, I mapped the lived environments of ND users: irregular routines, unpredictable energy, emotional volatility, and the need for flexible, non‑linear planning. Traditional calendars consistently triggered avoidance loops, guilt spirals, and disengagement. Neurocognitive models built for ADHD, ASD, PDA, and chronic‑fatigue profiles predicted that rigid structures would fail, and that users needed a system capable of adapting to their internal state rather than forcing them to adapt to the system.

To address this, I designed the first version of MAZE — a fractal, narrative‑based task architecture that decomposed goals into agent‑like branches, allowed zooming between micro‑actions and macro‑trajectories, and propagated emotional load across the structure. SelfScape became the first implementation of MAZE, validated in real ND daily conditions: inconsistent routines, sudden shutdowns, bursts of hyperfocus, multi‑day breaks, and irregular engagement. The system remained stable even when input data was chaotic, demonstrating that MAZE could support users without imposing rigid structure.

Although MAZE was created for neurodivergent contexts, its mechanisms proved far more general. A later project involving cognitive‑load management in language learning (Croatian) revealed that MAZE’s fractal direction mapping also benefits neurotypical users under stress, fatigue, or overload. The architecture scaled naturally beyond planning into identity‑level reasoning, long‑term trajectories, and narrative decision‑making.

As MAZE expanded, it became clear that it was not merely a planning tool — it was a life‑direction mapping system capable of representing goals, obligations, desires, and identity trajectories across time. This led to its formalisation as a standalone framework and its integration into the broader cognitive‑systems architecture. Today, MAZE serves as the directional layer for FEDRI and the structural foundation for IMI, enabling agents to navigate not only moment‑to‑moment cognition but also long‑range identity paths and evolving life narratives.
