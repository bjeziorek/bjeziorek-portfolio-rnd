Cognitive Modelling Behavioural Science Systems Thinking Neurocognition R&D Framework

SPINE is the core research philosophy that guides my work across cognitive R&D, behavioural design, and complex system environments. It combines systems thinking, predictive cognitive modelling, behavioural science, and real world validation to understand not only what users do, but why they do it — and how to design interventions that reliably change behaviour.

SPINE is designed for environments where behaviour is shaped by emotional load, cognitive constraints, structural limitations, or neurodivergent patterns. It provides a stable backbone for research in contexts where traditional UX methods are not enough.

Origin

SPINE emerged from eight years of practice before it ever had a name. I had been working in a SPINE‑like pattern since 2017, combining system grounding, cognitive modelling, friction mapping, and real‑world behavioural validation long before I formalised the method. The first large‑scale expression of this implicit approach appeared in MAKO, where the structure of SPINE was already visible, even if unnamed.

The turning point came in 2024–2025, during a high‑resolution behavioural forensics project. FEDRI successfully reconstructed a complex workplace conflict, but the absence of a formal grounding layer revealed a critical gap: powerful cognitive tools require equally powerful ethical and contextual safeguards. This experience made the need for a unified architecture unmistakable.

SPINE was formally defined in 2025 as the backbone that integrates all layers of cognitive R&D — Systems, Prediction, Intervention, Neurocognition, and Environment — transforming an intuitive method into a structured, ethical, and scalable framework

How SPINE Works

The Five Components

Systems
Understanding the system that shapes behaviour — its architecture, constraints, workflows, incentives, and cultural dynamics. This ensures that insights and interventions are grounded in reality rather than idealised assumptions.

Prediction
Building cognitive and behavioural models that forecast how users will respond under different conditions. Prediction allows me to test hypotheses before running real world experiments and to anticipate unintended consequences.

Intervention
Designing behavioural, emotional, or system level mechanisms that reduce friction and improve outcomes. Interventions are evaluated for feasibility, safety, and scalability before implementation.

Neurocognition
Modelling how users think, feel, process information, and regulate emotions — including both neurotypical and neurodivergent patterns. This ensures that solutions are emotionally safe and cognitively realistic.

Environment
Validating insights and interventions in the real conditions where behaviour occurs. This includes constraints, pressures, unpredictability, and the messy realities of everyday use.

Operational Workflow

The Six Stages That Implement SPINE

1. System Grounding

A deep understanding of the environment in which the user operates. This includes technical architecture, organisational constraints, workflow realities, and the “rules of the system” that shape behaviour.

  • mapping structural constraints (time, resources, regulations, legacy architecture)
  • identifying which parts of the system are rigid vs. adaptable
  • understanding real world limitations before defining research scope

Outcome: research that is realistic, context aware, and aligned with actual constraints.

2. Cognitive Modelling

Building predictive cognitive models of users based on emotional states, behavioural tendencies, and decision making patterns.

  • modelling emotional load, avoidance, anxiety, hyperfocus, motivation
  • creating multiple cognitive variants (e.g., high anxiety user, ND user, low energy user)
  • identifying triggers, blockers, and behavioural loops
  • mapping how internal states shape external actions

Outcome: a predictive understanding of how users will behave across contexts.

3. Behavioural Friction Mapping

Identifying where emotional or cognitive friction emerges and how it affects behaviour.

  • mapping overwhelm, avoidance, shutdown, disengagement
  • identifying hidden costs (e.g., stress reducing learning efficiency)
  • analysing behavioural bottlenecks and failure states
  • uncovering patterns invisible in quantitative data alone

Outcome: a clear map of where and why behaviour breaks down.

4. Hypothesis Architecture

Designing hypotheses and potential interventions grounded in cognitive and behavioural models.

  • defining which friction points are actionable
  • generating behavioural, emotional, or system level interventions
  • testing hypotheses against cognitive models before real world testing
  • iterating until the model predicts stable, safe outcomes

Outcome: interventions that are psychologically coherent and behaviourally plausible.

5. Mixed Methods Integration

Combining qualitative, quantitative, behavioural, and structural data into a unified insight model.

  • qualitative: interviews, observations, narrative analysis, emotional modelling
  • quantitative: surveys, telemetry, logs, A/B tests, behavioural metrics
  • behavioural: real world actions, adoption patterns, avoidance loops
  • structural: system constraints, architecture, workflows

Outcome: insights that are validated across multiple data types and grounded in real behaviour.

6. Systemic Implementation

Translating research into product decisions, prototypes, and long term strategy.

  • designing solutions aligned with cognitive models
  • evaluating technical feasibility and constraints
  • building prototypes (paper, interactive, or coded)
  • defining roadmaps and scaling strategies
  • validating impact through post launch behavioural data
  • maintaining a living knowledge repository

Outcome: research that directly shapes product direction and delivers measurable impact.
